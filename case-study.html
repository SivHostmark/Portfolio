Rethinking Udir.no
for everyone

Participatory design research for Norway's education directorate. Identifying why users struggle to navigate udir.no, and co-designing a way forward.

01 — Problem

What was the challenge?

Udir (the Norwegian Directorate for Education) is the government body responsible for kindergartens, primary and secondary education. Their website serves a remarkably broad audience: teachers, school leaders, parents, students, kindergarten staff, newcomers to Norway, and government administrators. But the website is quite difficult and confusing to navigate.

I chose Udir because it is a public website. People who need information here cannot easily go somewhere else, so it needs to work well for everyone.

"Udir er litt sånn … Her er ALT du ikke trenger på en gang."
("Udir is kind of like… here's EVERYTHING you don't need, all at once.")
— Student, 17

The Friction

Users couldn't predict where to click. Categories didn't follow a clear hierarchy, language was heavy with jargon, and multiple paths led to overlapping content, leaving people stuck on a page meant to guide them.

The Stakes

Udir is how Norway delivers on its public mandate for education. When a parent can't find their child's right to appeal an exam grade, or a teacher can't locate the regulations they need, the website isn't just inconvenient.

02 — Research & Approach

Understanding the people
and the process

I conducted semi-structured interviews with three participants from Udir's actual target audience: a 17-year-old upper secondary student, a 29-year-old parent with a child in kindergarten, and a 42-year-old primary school teacher. Each interview was followed by a low-fidelity co-design session where participants reshaped the front page themselves. Findings were analyzed through open coding on fragments and mapped to WCAG 2.1 success criteria.

Methods used
  • Semi-structured interviews
  • Participatory design sessions
  • Open coding (qualitative analysis)
  • WCAG 2.1 mapping
  • Low-fidelity co-design
Key insights

None of the participants felt the front page was built for them, despite all three being squarely in the target audience. The student went straight to search instead of navigation. The teacher called the category hierarchy illogical. The parent described the layout as unsettled and hard to scan.

03 — Design Process

From consepts
to tested iterations

Three concept directions emerged from the research, each addressing a different angle of the same core problem: Udir is trying to serve everyone on one page, and serving everyone means serving no one well. I sketched all three at low fidelity and tested them with participants through co-design sessions.

Critical design decision

I chose to let participants reshape the prototype themselves rather than evaluate a finished design. The pop-up I had drafted in advance turned out to look too polished — one participant didn't want to change anything, which taught me to keep prototypes visibly unfinished to invite real input.

What I cut

The "latest news" section came up in every interview as unnecessary on the front page. "Hvem går inn på udir for å lese nyheter?" asked the student. ("Who goes to udir to read the news?") It was removed from every concept direction.

04 — Solution

A front page that knows
who it's for

The strongest direction surfaced a target-group selector that lets users self-identify on arrival. Content then surfaces relevant to them — leaders and owners, staff in schools and kindergartens, students and parents, or newcomers to Norway. The selector replaces a generic front page with a guided one, while still letting users skip the choice if they prefer.

The co-design sessions surfaced something I didn't expect: participants disagreed about which variant was best. The teacher stripped the page back to almost nothing but the audience selector. The parent kept the pop-up but reorganized the rest of the page for symmetry. Both directions are defensible, and the disagreement itself is a finding — Udir would need to test variants with larger samples before committing.

Built for many audiences

The selector acknowledges what makes Udir hard in the first place: its audience is enormous and varied. Instead of hiding that complexity, the design lets users opt into the slice that's relevant to them.

Skippable, not forced

Users who know what they're looking for can dismiss the selector and use the page as before. The design adds a path without removing the existing one — a lower-risk change for a public-sector context.

05 — Reflections

What I learned

This project ended at low-fidelity by design — the brief was insight, not delivery. The participants gave me three things I'm taking forward: a sharper sense of how broad audiences fail when treated as one, a working method for combining participatory design with WCAG analysis, and a reminder that disagreement between users is a finding, not a failure.

I also chose Udir.no thinking I understood it. I didn't — its complexity caught me off guard, and that taught me to scope ambition to what can genuinely be investigated in the time available. If I were to take this further, I'd build a high-fidelity prototype of the strongest variant, A/B test task completion on real users, and coordinate with Udir's customer service to identify which inbound questions disappear when navigation improves — the most concrete measure of value a redesign could deliver.
