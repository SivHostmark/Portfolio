Rethinking Udir.no
for everyone

Participatory design research for Norway's education directorate, focused on why users struggle to navigate udir.no.

01 — Problem

What was the challenge?

Udir (the Norwegian Directorate for Education) is the government body responsible for kindergartens, primary and secondary education. Their website serves a broad audience: teachers, school leaders, parents, students, kindergarten staff, newcomers to Norway, and government administrators. But the website is quite difficult and confusing to navigate.

I chose Udir because it is a public website. People who need information here cannot easily go somewhere else, so it needs to work well for everyone.

"Udir er litt sånn … Her er ALT du ikke trenger på en gang."
("Udir is kind of like… here's EVERYTHING you don't need, all at once.")
— Student, 17

The Friction

Users couldn't predict where to click. Categories didn't follow a clear hierarchy, language was heavy with jargon, and multiple paths led to overlapping content, leaving people stuck on a page meant to guide them.

The Stakes

Udir is a public service responsible for education. When citizens can't navigate the site, they can't access information they have a right to, like how to appeal an exam grade or where to find regulations. For a public service, that's a real problem.

02 — Research & Approach

Understanding the people
and the process

Before starting, I emailed Udir's web editor and got permission to use the site for this project.

I conducted semi-structured interviews with three participants from Udir's target audience: a 17-year-old upper secondary student, a 29-year-old parent with a child in kindergarten, and a 42-year-old primary school teacher. After the interviews, I ran low-fidelity co-design sessions where participants could reshape the front page themselves. Findings were analyzed through open coding on fragments.

I also connected what I found to WCAG. Two rules stood out: 3.1.5 about reading level for the jargon, and 3.2.3 about navigation staying consistent for the confusing menus.

Three main themes came out of the coding: how the page looks and feels, language and jargon, and navigation.

How I ran the research
  • Semi-structured interviews
  • Participatory design sessions
  • Open coding (qualitative analysis)
  • WCAG 2.1 mapping
  • Low-fidelity co-design
What the participants showed me

The student went straight to search instead of trying to navigate, and didn't feel the page was built for them. The parent thought it looked tidy at first but reacted to the inconsistent symmetry. The teacher called the category hierarchy illogical. As one concrete example, "barnehagemiljø" was not placed under "barnehage".

"De burde fjerne alt og begynne på nytt."
("They should remove everything and start over.")
— Teacher, 42

03 — Design Process

Sketching and testing

Based on the interviews, I outlined three concept directions, and developed the first as a low-fi prototype for testing. All three explored the same approach: letting users choose their audience so the page surfaces content relevant to them. The aim was to reduce the cognitive load participants described.

The three directions were a pop-up selector on entry, the same selector placed at the top of the page, and an improved version of the existing category bar already on the site.

Choosing not to lead the user

I chose to let participants reshape the prototype themselves rather than evaluate a finished design. The pop-up I had drafted in advance turned out to look too polished. One participant didn't want to change anything, which taught me to keep prototypes visibly unfinished to invite real input.

What I cut

The "latest news" section came up in every interview as unnecessary on the front page. "Hvem går inn på udir for å lese nyheter?" asked the student. ("Who goes to udir to read the news?") It was removed from every concept direction.

I started with a low-fidelity prototype built around a pop-up category selector. I brought it to each co-design session as a starting point for participants to reshape.

Starting point — my initial low-fi prototype.

The student could not take part in the co-design session due to illness, so the testing happened with the parent and the teacher. They engaged with the prototype differently. The parent liked the pop-up and kept it unchanged, but redesigned the rest of the front page focusing on symmetry. The teacher set my idea aside and built his own version with simpler categories: student, teacher, and parent.

04 — Solution

Letting users choose
their audience

The three directions I outlined all built on the same idea: a target-group selector that lets users self-identify so the page surfaces content relevant to them. The four audience groups were:

  • Leaders and owners
  • Staff in schools and kindergartens
  • Students and parents
  • Newcomers to Norway

Users could skip the choice if they preferred. Only the first was developed as a prototype and tested in co-design sessions. More rounds of testing would be needed to know which direction works best.

The co-design sessions showed how differently each participant engaged with the prototype. The teacher rejected the pop-up and built his own version with simpler categories (student, teacher, parent) on top of Udir's existing category bar. The parent kept the pop-up unchanged and instead reorganized the front page for symmetry. Both responses are useful findings, but the design directions would need more rounds of testing before Udir could commit to one.

Built for many audiences

Udir's audience is broad and varied. The selector lets users opt into the part that's relevant to them.

Skippable, not forced

Users who know what they're looking for can dismiss the selector and use the page as before. Nothing is removed.

05 — Reflections

What worked,
what didn't

I focused on collecting insight rather than designing a finished solution. The project gave me a deeper understanding of participatory design, universal design and WCAG.

I also chose Udir.no thinking I understood it. I didn't. Its complexity caught me off guard, and that taught me to be realistic about what I can actually investigate in the time I have. If I were to take this further, I'd complete the low-fi test with the student I missed, and then build a high-fidelity prototype to user test with the participants.
